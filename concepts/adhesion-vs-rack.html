Adhesion vs. Rack

why steel wheels slip — and what beats them
SourceWikipedia — Adhesion railway ↗

Steel wheels on steel rail are the most efficient way we know to move a heavy load over land — and that efficiency is exactly why a train can't climb a steep hill. The same slickness that lets a locomotive coast for miles on one push lets its wheels spin uselessly the moment the track tilts too far up.

The friction ceiling

A train moves because friction between wheel and rail turns the engine's torque into forward push. But steel-on-steel is a low-friction pairing by design — that's why one locomotive can haul thousands of tonnes on modest power. The catch: the grip that pulls you along a level track is the same grip that must hold you on a slope. Tilt the track up and gravity drags the train backward; once that pull beats what friction can resist, the wheels break traction and spin in place. Efficiency and climbing ability are two sides of one coin.

Where the ceiling actually sits

How much grip is there? Engineers call it the coefficient of adhesion — the fraction of a driven wheelset's weight that becomes forward force. On clean, dry rail it's about 0.35 (up to ~0.5 in good conditions, 0.78 in the lab). But wet rail, frost, grease, or crushed autumn leaves can drop it toward 0.05 — a seventh of the dry figure.

That number sets the grade limit directly. Ordinary main lines stay gentle: freight routes aim for under 1.5%, and even high-speed lines rarely top 2.5–4% — both for haulage and to survive a wet day. A determined mountain adhesion line can push to roughly 7–10%. The steepest ever built top out near 11–14%: Lisbon's Calçada de São Francisco tram climbs 13.8% (1 in 7.2), the Pöstlingbergbahn 11.6%. Past that, no amount of sand or wheelslip control saves you — the wheels just spin.

Dropping dry sand onto the railhead lifts the coefficient a notch and is standard kit on steep starts — but it only nudges the ceiling, it doesn't remove it.

Three ways to beat it

When the mountain is steeper than friction allows, you stop relying on friction. Three moves:

  • A rack rail. A toothed rail between the running rails, meshed by a cogwheel under the train. Teeth don't slip. The rack railway on Mount Pilatus grips a 48% grade — the world's steepest — and rack systems can in principle exceed 100% (a 45° slope).
  • A cable. Let a stationary winch pull instead of the wheels. A funicular hauls its car uphill on a rope and shrugs off grades of 110%+, because rope tension — not wheel grip — carries the load.
  • An aerial ropeway. Hang the cabin from a cable between towers and skip the ground entirely; gradient stops being a limit at all.

Flåm: living just under the edge

Norway's Flåm Railway is the exception that proves the rule. It's famous for being punishingly steep — yet it's a pure adhesion line: no rack, no cable, just steel on steel. Its ruling grade is 5.5% (1 in 18) for nearly 80% of its length, about as steep as a standard adhesion railway dares to go in Norwegian weather. It sits deliberately under the ~7–10% ceiling, leaning on heavy multi-system braking rather than exotic traction — the honest limit of what steel-on-steel can do.
